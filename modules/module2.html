Common Cyber Threats

Understanding the threats that can compromise your information security

Learning Objectives

  • Identify various types of cyber threats
  • Recognize signs of phishing and social engineering
  • Understand how malware spreads and infects systems
  • Learn preventive measures against common attacks

Introduction

In this module, we'll explore the most common cyber threats and attacks that organizations face today. Understanding these threats is the first step toward protecting yourself and your organization. By the end of this module, you'll be able to identify various attack methods and know how to respond appropriately.

Types of Cyber Threats

Understanding the landscape of cyber threats

External Threats

  • Hackers: Individuals or groups seeking unauthorized access
  • Cybercriminals: Motivated by financial gain
  • Nation-state actors: Government-sponsored attacks
  • Hacktivists: Politically or socially motivated

Internal Threats

  • Malicious insiders: Employees with harmful intent
  • Negligent employees: Unintentional security breaches
  • Compromised accounts: Legitimate accounts used maliciously
  • Third-party vendors: External partners with access

Threat Landscape Evolution

The cyber threat landscape is constantly evolving. Attackers continuously develop new techniques and exploit emerging technologies. Organizations must stay vigilant and adapt their security measures to address these evolving threats.

Phishing Attacks

Deceptive attempts to steal sensitive information

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of social engineering attack where cybercriminals impersonate legitimate organizations to trick individuals into revealing sensitive information such as passwords, credit card numbers, or personal data.

These attacks typically come through email, text messages, or fake websites that appear legitimate but are designed to steal your information.

Common Phishing Tactics

  • Urgent action required messages
  • Fake security alerts
  • Prize or lottery notifications
  • Account verification requests
  • Fake invoices or receipts

Warning Signs

  • Generic greetings ("Dear Customer")
  • Spelling and grammar errors
  • Suspicious sender addresses
  • Unexpected attachments
  • Mismatched URLs

How to Protect Yourself

  • Verify sender identity through independent channels
  • Never click suspicious links or download unexpected attachments
  • Type URLs directly into your browser
  • Use multi-factor authentication
  • Report suspicious emails to IT security

Social Engineering

Manipulating people to divulge confidential information

What is Social Engineering?

Social engineering is the art of manipulating people to divulge confidential information or perform actions that compromise security. Rather than breaking in or finding software vulnerabilities, social engineers exploit human psychology and trust.

Common Techniques

  • Pretexting: Creating false scenarios to gain trust
  • Baiting: Offering something enticing to spark curiosity
  • Quid pro quo: Offering services in exchange for information
  • Tailgating: Following someone into restricted areas

Psychological Triggers

  • Authority (impersonating executives)
  • Urgency (creating time pressure)
  • Fear (threatening consequences)
  • Curiosity (intriguing offers)

Defense Strategies

  • Verify identity before sharing information
  • Be skeptical of unsolicited requests
  • Follow established procedures for information sharing
  • When in doubt, consult with security team

Malware and Ransomware

Malicious software designed to harm or exploit systems

Types of Malware

  • Viruses: Self-replicating programs
  • Worms: Spread across networks
  • Trojans: Disguised malicious software
  • Spyware: Secretly monitors activities
  • Adware: Displays unwanted advertisements
  • Rootkits: Hide deep in system files

Ransomware: A Growing Threat

Ransomware is a type of malware that encrypts files on infected systems and demands payment for the decryption key. It has become one of the most significant cyber threats to organizations worldwide.

How it spreads:
  • • Email attachments
  • • Malicious websites
  • • USB drives
  • • Network vulnerabilities
Impact:
  • • Data encryption/loss
  • • Business disruption
  • • Financial losses
  • • Reputation damage

Prevention Measures

  • Keep software and operating systems updated
  • Use reputable antivirus software
  • Regular data backups
  • Avoid suspicious downloads and attachments
  • Implement network segmentation

Real-World Examples & Summary

Learning from actual cyber incidents

Notable Cyber Attacks

WannaCry Ransomware (2017)

Affected over 300,000 computers worldwide, exploiting Windows vulnerabilities.

Equifax Data Breach (2017)

Personal information of 147 million people compromised due to unpatched software.

SolarWinds Supply Chain Attack (2020)

Sophisticated attack affecting thousands of organizations through software updates.

Key Takeaways

  • Cyber threats are constantly evolving and becoming more sophisticated
  • Human factors are often the weakest link in cybersecurity
  • Prevention is more cost-effective than recovery
  • Regular training and awareness are essential
  • Everyone has a role to play in cybersecurity
